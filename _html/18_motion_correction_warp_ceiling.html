Question

The trained VGGT+refiner reconstructs the 3-D heart at a queried cardiac phase by predicting a per-pixel displacement field Δ that warps ~10 scattered single-frame input slices, splatting their intensities into a 12×256×256 canonical cube (world_points = scanner_coords + Δ → trilinear coverage-averaged splat → V_canon; loss = L1 + TV on V_canon, SSIM on the refiner). It under-corrects motion (the headline metric, motion PSNR on dynamic heart voxels, sits at 20.6 dB, far below a perfect-placement oracle at 35 dB).

What is the best way to improve motion correction? Specifically: should we drop splatting? change the architecture? the training dynamics? the loss? — settled by experiment, not opinion.

Conclusion (TL;DR)

The motion-correction bottleneck is the WARP-ONLY ARCHITECTURE, not motion estimation, the loss, or training dynamics. A perfectly optimized, target-aware warp of the real input slices — no network, no r-blindness, every Δ parameterization from rigid to free-per-pixel — tops out at ~21 dB motion PSNR, essentially equal to the trained model (20.6) and ~14 dB below the oracle (35). The model is already at its architectural ceiling. That 14 dB is the appearance wall: warping other-phase input intensities cannot synthesize target-phase appearance (end-systolic blood-pool, through-plane disocclusion); the oracle only reaches 35 because it is handed the true target-phase planes. So the highest-ROI direction is appearance synthesis — replace/augment the pure intensity-warp-splat with a learned renderer/decoder — NOT better motion estimation, loss tweaks, or phase conditioning. (This corrects an earlier conclusion of mine that "~14 dB is motion-estimation headroom" — that was an artifact of comparing to an oracle no warp can reach.)

Confidence: high that the warp ceiling ≈ the model ≪ the oracle (cross-validated across 5 Δ parameterizations and an independent clean-LR run, n=4 subjects). The proposed fix (a learned appearance decoder) is the indicated next step, not yet trained — see Confidence.

The toy

To separate "the objective/renderer caps motion" from "the network/optimizer is the gap," I removed the 941M network entirely and directly optimized the displacement field Δ on a real breathing-val sample, against the true target volume, through the same splat. This is the warp-only ceiling: the best any warp+splat can do given the real inputs and a perfect (target-aware) optimizer. Two toys:

  • E0 — loss-landscape sweep (no optimizer). Inputs = a single phase's own planes at aligned

positions (so zero offset is a perfect reconstruction); sweep a known rigid offset along through-plane z vs in-plane x and record the loss. Measures the objective's gradient conditioning directly, immune to optimizer/LR confounds.

  • E1–E4 — direct-Δ optimization (rigid / low-rank control-grid / free-per-pixel) to minimize

L1(splat, V_gt), measuring the achievable motion PSNR = the warp ceiling.

Faithfulness check: at zero offset E0 reconstructs the target near-perfectly (L1≈0.05 on motion voxels), and direct-opt at a sane LR reaches exactly the trained model's 20.6 dB — so the toy reproduces the real regime, not a caricature.

Hypotheses & debate

Three adversarial subagents argued competing causes and designed the discriminating tests:

  • Objective/renderer-limited (steelman): the splat's coverage-division + 12-plane/8 mm z-axis

make the through-plane gradient flat → under-correction is baked into the objective. Prediction: in E0, z under-recovers vs x.

  • Network/information-limited (steelman): the objective is fine; the gap is r-blindness

(conditional-mean shrinkage, doc 07 slope 0.42) + frozen natural-image features. Prediction: direct-opt (target-aware) recovers the motion the blind model can't.

  • Red-team: direct-opt sees the target so it cannot measure the information limit — only the

objective's optimizability; trust the no-optimizer E0 landscape, not Adam's endpoint; beware the coverage-division faking flat z-gradients and free-Δ degenerate fits.

The red-team's keystone (E0) + the warp-ceiling partition became the decisive experiments.

Experiments & results

E0 — the splat objective is well-posed but ~2× ill-conditioned through-plane

The through-plane (z) loss-gradient is ~2× weaker than in-plane (x), and the splat'shigh

coverage-division is a culprit. At matched physical mm, L1 rises 0.0067/mm in z vs 0.0143/mm in x (z is 2.1× flatter near the optimum → a gradient learner under-corrects z proportionally). Removing the coverage-division (V=acc/(cov+ε) → raw accumulate) or using an inverse-warp renderer restores the z-gradient to 0.0103/mm (~1.5×). Verified clean by prove-it (coverage maxes at 1.0 here, so this is a real conditioning effect, not a scale artifact; mm↔normalized conversion is physically fair). The minimum is at the true alignment for both axes — the objective is well-posed, just ill-conditioned in z.

E1/E3 — the first "objective is misaligned" conclusion was a BUG (caught by prove-it)

My initial direct-opt runs (lr=0.03) showed L1 going down while motion PSNR collapsed below the identity floor, which I read as "minimizing L1 hurts motion → the objective is misaligned." prove-it refuted this: at lr=0.03 Adam diverges — the free Δ explodes to a ~40-voxel mean displacement, points splat out of bounds, and L1 actually goes UP (0.014→0.051). It was never minimizing L1. At lr=0.005, L1 is genuinely minimized (→0.0054) and motion PSNR rises to 20.6 = the trained model. So L1 and motion PSNR are aligned, the opposite of my first claim. The corrected experiment below uses sane per-parameterization LRs + a clamp + convergence logging.

Warp-only ceiling — ≈ the model, ~14 dB below the oracle (the decisive result)

A perfectly-optimized, target-aware warp tops out at ~21 dB motion PSNR — the trained model ishigh

already there. identity 16.8 → model 20.6 → warp ceiling ~21 → oracle 35. The model→warp-ceiling gap is ~0.5 dB (the model has essentially solved the warp problem, despite being r-blind), while the warp-ceiling→oracle gap is ~14 dB. Since the oracle is built from the true target-phase planes and the warp only rearranges other-phase input intensities, that 14 dB is the appearance wall, not motion estimation.

The ceiling is the warp, not the Δ representation.high

rigid (3 params/slot) 19.0, low-rank G16 20.6, G32 21.2, free-per-pixel 20.8, free+TV 20.9 — every parameterization lands at ~19-21 dB. Richer Δ does not help, because the limit is the appearance content available in the inputs, which no displacement field can change. (Independently, the prove-it reviewer's clean lr=0.005 run reached 20.6, cross-validating the ceiling.)

What this rules out

Better motion estimation, loss changes, and phase conditioning have small upside for motion PSNR.medium

The model is at the warp ceiling, so: (a) loss — L1 is aligned with motion (E1/E3 corrected), and no loss can make a warp synthesize absent appearance; (b) conditioning / r-blindness — knowing the target (incl. respiratory state) buys only ~0.5 dB in the warp regime (warp-ceiling 21 vs model 20.6), so r-blindness is not the dominant cost here; (c) better Δ / motion estimation — capped at ~21. These can help the model reach the ceiling more robustly but cannot break it.

Confirmation at scale

The warp-ceiling experiment runs on the real model regime — real CMRxRecon breathing-val samples, the production splat and canonical grid, the real input slices (other-phase, breathing-corrupted) — across 5 Δ parameterizations × 4 subjects, and is cross-validated by an independent clean-LR direct-opt (→20.6). The E0 conditioning result is n=8 subjects and verified bug-free by prove-it.

What remains unverified: the fix itself. The conclusion proves the warp ceiling is ~21 dB and that appearance synthesis is required to exceed it; it does not yet measure how far a learned renderer/decoder actually gets — that needs a training run (scoped below). Also: n=4 for the ceiling is small (subject variance), and the direct-opt optimization is mildly unstable (motion PSNR peaks then drifts under the clamp), so ~21 is reported as an upper bound on the warp ceiling — which only strengthens the "≪ 35" conclusion.

Conclusion & confidence

Proved (high confidence): the warp+splat architecture's motion-PSNR ceiling is ~21 dB ≈ the trained model, ~14 dB below the perfect-placement oracle; that gap is the appearance wall (warping other-phase intensities cannot synthesize target-phase appearance), not motion estimation. The splat's coverage-division additionally weakens the through-plane gradient ~2× (a secondary, reach-the-ceiling lever, fixable with a coverage-free / inverse-warp renderer).

Therefore — the best way to improve motion correction, ranked:

  1. Appearance synthesis (the only thing that breaks ~21 dB). Move beyond pure intensity transport:

splat features (not a single intensity) + a small 3-D decoder, or a learned/implicit renderer that can generate target-phase appearance. This is the dominant lever; expected to be the only one that closes a meaningful fraction of the 14 dB. Gate against hallucination on held-out subjects.

  1. Coverage-free / inverse-warp renderer (replace V=acc/(cov+ε)): restores the through-plane

gradient (E0), helping the model and any future decoder reach the ceiling; cheap, but PSNR-bounded by ~21 until #1 is done.

  1. Do NOT prioritize loss re-engineering, free-vs-low-rank Δ, unfreezing, or phase conditioning

for motion PSNR — proven near-zero headroom against the warp ceiling. (They remain relevant to sharpness and OOD robustness, separate axes.)

Expected, not proved: that a feature-splat + decoder will recover a large share of the 14 dB without hallucinating — the next experiment to run.
